Lung Cancer

Understanding lung cancer: types, symptoms, risk factors, and treatment options for this serious condition.

Visual Comparison of Healthy Lungs vs. Cancerous Lungs

üß¨ Introduction

Lung cancer is a type of cancer that begins in the lungs and occurs when cells grow uncontrollably, forming tumors that interfere with lung function. It's the leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide. There are two main types: non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) which accounts for about 85% of cases, and small cell lung cancer (SCLC) which grows more quickly and is strongly associated with smoking.

ü´Å How It Affects the Lungs

Lung cancer typically starts in the cells lining the bronchi and other parts of the lung. As cancer grows, it can:

  • Block airways, causing breathing difficulties
  • Invade nearby tissues and spread to lymph nodes
  • Metastasize to other organs like the brain, bones, or liver
  • Cause fluid accumulation around the lungs (pleural effusion)

Did you know? About 1 in 16 people will be diagnosed with lung cancer in their lifetime, with over 230,000 new cases annually in the U.S. alone.

üß™ Risk Factors

Primary risk factors for developing lung cancer:

Smoking

Accounts for about 80-90% of lung cancer deaths

Radon Exposure

Second leading cause (odorless radioactive gas)

Occupational Hazards

Asbestos, arsenic, diesel exhaust, some chemicals

Family History

Genetic predisposition in some cases

Air Pollution

Long-term exposure to polluted air

Previous Radiation Therapy

Especially to the chest for other cancers

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention & Screening

Smoking Cessation

  • Quit smoking - benefits begin immediately
  • Avoid secondhand smoke exposure
  • Use FDA-approved cessation aids if needed

Environmental Control

  • Test your home for radon
  • Follow workplace safety protocols
  • Avoid outdoor activities when air quality is poor

Healthy Lifestyle

  • Eat a diet rich in fruits and vegetables
  • Exercise regularly
  • Limit alcohol consumption

Screening

  • Annual low-dose CT scans for high-risk individuals
  • Ages 50-80 with 20 pack-year smoking history
  • Current smokers or those who quit within past 15 years

üè• Treatment Options

Surgery

For early-stage cancers (lobectomy, segmentectomy, wedge resection)

Radiation Therapy

External beam radiation or stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT)

Chemotherapy

Often used before or after surgery, or for advanced cases

Targeted Therapy

For cancers with specific genetic mutations (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, etc.)

Immunotherapy

Checkpoint inhibitors (Keytruda, Opdivo) to boost immune response

Palliative Care

To relieve symptoms and improve quality of life
