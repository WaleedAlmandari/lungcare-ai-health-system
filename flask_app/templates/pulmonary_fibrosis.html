Pulmonary Fibrosis

Understanding pulmonary fibrosis: symptoms, causes, and management of this chronic lung scarring condition.

Comparison of Healthy Lungs vs. Fibrotic Lungs

üß¨ Introduction

Pulmonary fibrosis is a chronic, progressive lung disease where lung tissue becomes damaged and scarred, making it increasingly difficult to breathe. The thickened, stiff tissue makes it harder for oxygen to pass into the bloodstream. Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is the most common type, meaning the cause is unknown. The disease typically worsens over time, though progression varies between individuals.

ü´Å How It Affects the Lungs

In pulmonary fibrosis, the delicate air sacs (alveoli) and lung tissue become thickened and scarred (fibrotic). This scarring:

  • Reduces lung elasticity and capacity
  • Creates a honeycomb-like appearance on imaging
  • Impairs oxygen transfer to the bloodstream
  • Leads to progressive respiratory failure

Did you know? Pulmonary fibrosis affects about 100,000 people in the U.S., with 30,000-40,000 new cases diagnosed annually.

Shortness of Breath

Progressive difficulty breathing (dyspnea)

Dry Cough

Persistent, hacking cough without mucus

Fatigue

Persistent tiredness and weakness

Weight Loss

Unexplained loss of appetite and weight

Muscle/Joint Pain

Aching in muscles and joints

Clubbing

Widening and rounding of fingertips/nails

üß™ Causes & Risk Factors

Potential causes and contributing factors:

Idiopathic

No known cause (IPF - Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis)

Environmental

Long-term exposure to toxins (silica, asbestos, coal dust)

Radiation

Radiation therapy to the chest

Medications

Certain chemotherapy drugs, heart medications

Autoimmune

Rheumatoid arthritis, scleroderma, sarcoidosis

Genetic

Family history increases risk

üõ°Ô∏è Management & Treatment

Medications

  • Antifibrotic drugs (nintedanib, pirfenidone)
  • Corticosteroids for inflammation
  • Acid reflux medications
  • Oxygen therapy as needed

Therapies

  • Pulmonary rehabilitation
  • Supplemental oxygen
  • Lung transplant evaluation
  • Vaccinations (pneumonia, flu)

Lifestyle

  • Smoking cessation (critical)
  • Regular gentle exercise
  • Healthy, nutrient-rich diet
  • Breathing techniques

Prevention

  • Avoid smoking and secondhand smoke
  • Protective equipment for hazardous jobs
  • Manage autoimmune conditions
  • Regular medical checkups if at risk

üè• When to Seek Medical Care

Seek immediate medical attention for:

  • Sudden worsening of breathing
  • Blue lips or fingernails (cyanosis)
  • Extreme fatigue or confusion
  • Rapid, shallow breathing
  • Chest pain or pressure
